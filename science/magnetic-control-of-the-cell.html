alessandrolodesani 9/12/2025 12:15 PM
Great questions! The way we envision magnetic field control of the cell is, rather than going for highly localized magnetic field, understanding how different magnetic fields (in strength, frequency and direction) affect different proteins and cellular processes. We do believe that every protein will respond differently and will have an optimized activation/deactivation corresponding to certain field parameters. Once we figure out the field parameters needed to drive a certain effect, it's only a matter of applying that specific field (which doesn't need to be extremely localized since it will target mostly the process we're interested in). Of course, one can also engineer proteins to enhance the response and induce more dramatic effects. Hope this makes sense.. happy to answer further questions!

You can find a list of a bunch of peer reviewed studies that investigate magnetic field effects in biology here: https://bit.ly/MFEbiology . There is a bunch of data that indicates that magnetic fields can be used for a large variety of applications. The problem is that the data ranges over a bunch of different field intensities, frequencies, directions, patterns of applications so it doesn't paint a coherent picture of the mechanism of interaction. Also, the quality of the studies is not very consistent. Our goal is to approach this rigorously and systematically and dive deep into the mechanism, keeping in mind that if we understand how the fields are sensed by biology, we'll be able to deterministically tailor magnetic fields to specific applications.

Chris Rourk 3/26/2026 10:42 AM
There is much evidence that ferritin naturally has bioelectric and biomagnetic properties. Altering it such as to make magnetoferritin and then adding it to a cell introduces something into the cell that’s not already there. What’s missing is any study of the effect of magnetic fields on ferritin separate from cryptochrome or anything else in the cell. There is some recent evidence that shows that ferritin in the cell might generate a magnetic field consistent with Ron Naaman’s work. IMHO, focusing on cryptochrome is probably not going to yield the answer to magnetosensation. It hasn’t for decades. Attaching magnetoferritin to ligands will not remove the native ferritin from the cell. Magnetogenomics testing has shown calcium signaling arising from both native ferritin and added ferritin that is attached to ion channels in response to RF stimulation.
