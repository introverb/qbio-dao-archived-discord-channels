Now, let’s go back to the basics for anyone who’s new to quantum biology. What is quantum biology? It’s the study of how quantum physics—principles like particles acting as waves, entanglement (how particles stay connected even over long distances), and tunneling (how particles pass through barriers)—affects biological systems. While traditional biology focuses on cells and molecules, quantum biology explores what’s happening at the smallest, quantum level of life (going even deeper than the cells). Why does it matter? Understanding quantum biology could lead to breakthroughs in medicine, sustainable energy, and new technologies. It gives us a deeper understanding of life itself and opens up exciting possibilities for the future. For example, we've discovered that weak magnetic fields can influence living things in surprising ways. They can affect how embryos develop, how DNA repairs itself, how energy is used in cells (metabolism), and even how cells handle stress... And this is just the beginning. Spin (1) Stern-Gerlach experiment Originally, "spin" was posited as the result of the Stern-Gerlach experiment. If you fire things like electrons through a non-uniform magnetic field, the electrical component of the electrons overpowers the magnetic component and you don't see a special isolated effect. Stern and Gerlach thought to use silver atoms instead, since silver atoms are electrically neutral. The result they got was that the beam of atoms split into two, so that there were two clusters on the detector rather than one. Since the electrical component had bee controlled for, the only remaining explanation was magnetic. It was then posited that silver atoms, and other particles, have a feature called "spin" which determines whether they swerve one way or another when they enter a non-uniform magnetic field. Using a classical analogy, one can think about what happens if a spinning top encounters a ramp. As it goes up the ramp, whether the top is spinning left or right will make the top swerve right or left as it starts to go up the ramp. This raises the question of whether it makes sense to think about "spin" using classical analogies. Certainly the word spin comes from a classical analogy, but the issue is a bit more complicated. (2) Electron spin It was eventually determined that many different particles, and systems of particles, have spin. Electrons, for instance, have spin of either +1/2 or -1/2. The fact that electrons have spin, however, raises some issues about what spin actually is. Physicists sometimes posit that electrons are point particles, meaning, they occupy only a point in space. We don't have experimental information about the interior of an electron, so the idea that electrons are point-sized provides a way out of postulating an unknown internal dynamics. (3) What is spin? If we think about spin classically, then it is the intrinsic angular momentum of a system or particle. One can then use regular analogies, etc., though of course one will have to dea with superpositions when those arise, since particles with spin can become entangled with one another. If we think about spin non-classically, then (in answer to your question @Gavin), then spin indicates a different direction, like you said, but that direction isn't a direction that pertains to velocity. Rather, it's a direction that pertains to how its trajectory bends when it encounters a magnetic field. One challenge in thinking about spin is that if one actually has a free-floating classical top in space, it spinning "left" or "right" or "up" or "down" depends on the orientation of the observer. However, one can use the analogy of "handedness" aka chirality. Objects with chirality (or handedness) cannot be put in the place of their reflections. Mittens do not have chirality but gloves do, since a mitten can be moved over into the position of its reflection, whereas a right-handed glove cannot be put into the place of a left-handed glove. Similarly, a classical spinning top spinning one way cannot be put in the place of a duplicate spinning top spinning the other way. (For those who like spacetime diagrams, the spacetime diagram of a 3-dimensional left-spinning top is a 4-dimensional spiral with chirality. So a 3-d top spinning one way cannot be put in the place of a 3-d top spinning the other way.) There's more to be said about this, since (as in quantum biology) one sometimes talks about triplet states where one state has intrinsic angular momentum of 1 but spin of 0. That has to do with the chosen basis and the projection of the intrinsic angular momentum vector onto the z-axis — but for regular purposes, this nuance may be ignored.
